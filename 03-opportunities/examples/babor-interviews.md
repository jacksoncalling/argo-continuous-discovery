# Examples

Three triage examples from a Product Manager at **BABOR BEAUTY GROUP** — a family-owned,
vertically integrated German cosmetics company (own R&D, production at the 60,000m²
Beauty Cluster in Aachen, own retail stores, beauty spas, e-commerce, professional
partner network, and Private Label/full-service for hotels, dermatologists, and startups).
She operates in 70+ countries.

Her current product outcome: **Increase repeat purchase rate among online customers by 15%.**

She interviews across the value chain: end consumers, beauty professionals (spa managers,
independent estheticians), internal teams (R&D, sales), and B2B clients. The operator
must handle this diversity — and recognize when a "product opportunity" is actually a
channel conflict.

---

## Example 1: Standard Triage — Spa Manager Interview

### Raw Interview Notes

> Interviewed Maria, spa manager at a Babor Beauty Spa in Düsseldorf. Been using Babor
> products for 8 years. She orders products through the Professional Partner Portal and
> uses the Derma Visualizer (80x magnification camera) for in-store skin analysis. Results
> go into each client's Beauty Pass.
>
> She said her clients often ask her which products to use at home between treatments.
> She recommends specific products — usually from DOCTOR BABOR or the HSR line — but
> then the client goes home and can't remember what she said. "They text me photos of
> products asking 'is this the one?' — it happens maybe three times a week."
>
> She tried writing product names on the back of her business card but that gets lost.
> She mentioned that the online Hautanalyse (AI skin analysis tool) gives clients a
> completely different recommendation than what she prescribed. "I build a routine based
> on the Derma Visualizer and the Beauty Pass, and then they go home, do the online
> Hautanalyse, and get told to buy something else entirely. It confuses them."
>
> She also said she wishes the professional portal would let her see which of her clients
> actually purchased what she recommended. "I want to know if my advice is landing."
>
> When I asked about competitors, she said some of her clients bring in products from
> The Ordinary and ask her to incorporate them. She doesn't mind but it makes her job
> harder because she can't vouch for the interaction between products.

### Operator Output

**INTERVIEW SNAPSHOT**

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Interviewee** | Spa manager, Düsseldorf, 8 years with Babor |
| **Segment** | Professional channel — spa operator |
| **Context** | Orders via professional portal, recommends to end clients |

**Experience Map:**
1. Client receives treatment → Maria builds personalized routine
2. Client leaves spa → cannot remember product recommendations
3. Client texts Maria with product photos → 3x per week
4. Client visits website → starts from zero, no connection to spa visit
5. Maria has no visibility into whether client followed through

**Extracted Opportunities:**

| # | Opportunity | Type | Phase | Confidence | Route |
|---|------------|------|-------|------------|-------|
| 1 | Clients cannot recall product recommendations after leaving the spa | Pain Point | Core Use → Purchase | Emerging | **ADD TO TREE** — novel, sits under "Post-treatment purchase conversion" |
| 2 | Online Hautanalyse (AI skin tool) contradicts the in-spa Derma Visualizer / Beauty Pass recommendations, confusing clients | Pain Point | Evaluation | Emerging | **ADD TO TREE** — novel. Two Babor tools giving conflicting advice is a system-level problem. |
| 3 | No connection between in-spa recommendation and online shopping experience | Pain Point | Purchase | Emerging | **ADD TO TREE** — potential parent opportunity for #1 and #2 |
| 4 | Spa managers have no visibility into whether their recommendations convert to purchases | Need | Loyalty | Emerging | **NEEDS MORE INTERVIEWS** — is this a widespread professional need or Maria-specific? Follow-up: ask 2 more spa managers. |
| 5 | ~~"Portal should let me see client purchases"~~ → Reframed: spa managers need feedback on whether their professional advice translates to client action | Need | Loyalty | Emerging | **REFRAME** — original was a solution (portal feature). Underlying need is the feedback loop. |

**Solution Signals (noted, not acted on):**
- Business card with handwritten product names (Maria's current workaround)
- "Some kind of link between my recommendation and their online cart" (Maria's suggestion)
- The Beauty Pass already captures the analysis — the gap is that it doesn't travel with the client to the online shop

**Memorable Quote:**
> "I do all this work building a routine for them, and then they go to the website and they're starting from zero."

**Follow-up Questions for Next Interview:**
- Ask other spa managers: "After you recommend products, do your clients follow through? How do you know?"
- Ask online customers: "Have you ever visited a Babor spa? Did your spa experience influence what you bought online?"

## Example 2: Edge Case — Solution-Only Interview

### Raw Interview Notes

> Interviewed Klaus, 45, IT manager, buys Babor online for his wife as gifts. Has
> purchased 4 times in the last year.
>
> "You need a better gift finder on the website. Something where I put in what she
> likes and it tells me what to buy."
>
> "The product names mean nothing to me. SeaCreation, HY-ÖL, DOCTOR BABOR — I don't
> know what any of this is. I just want to pick something nice for under 80 euros."
>
> "I usually end up buying the same ampoule set because I know she liked it. I'd try
> something new but I don't want to get it wrong."
>
> "Last time I tried the Produktberater and it asked me for her skin type — empfindliche
> Haut, fettige Haut, Mischhaut — I have no idea. Then it asked which concerns she has:
> Anti-Aging, Rötungen, Pigmentflecken... I just closed the tab."
>
> "I also tried to get it gift-wrapped and apparently that's not possible. For a premium
> brand at this price point, that surprised me."
>
> When asked if he'd read product descriptions: "I skimmed them. They're written for
> people who already know about skincare. I need the version for someone who doesn't."

### Operator Output

**INTERVIEW SNAPSHOT**

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Interviewee** | Gift buyer, 45, buys 4x/year for partner |
| **Segment** | Online — non-expert gift purchaser |
| **Context** | Repeat buyer but stuck in safe choices, intimidated by product knowledge requirements |

**Experience Map:**
1. Decides to buy gift → arrives at website
2. Faces product catalog → names and categories are meaningless
3. Tries to explore → product descriptions assume skincare knowledge
4. Encounters skin type question at checkout → abandons cart
5. Falls back to repurchasing same product → safe but boring

**Extracted Opportunities:**

| # | Opportunity | Type | Phase | Confidence | Route |
|---|------------|------|-------|------------|-------|
| 1 | Non-expert buyers cannot navigate the product catalog without skincare knowledge | Pain Point | Evaluation | Emerging | **ADD TO TREE** — novel. Sits under "Purchase barriers for non-primary users" |
| 2 | Product descriptions assume existing skincare literacy | Pain Point | Evaluation | Emerging | **NEEDS MORE INTERVIEWS** — is this specific to gift buyers or also affecting skincare beginners? |
| 3 | The Produktberater requires skincare knowledge (skin type, concerns) that gift buyers don't have — causes cart abandonment | Pain Point | Purchase | Emerging | **ADD TO TREE** — this is a cart abandonment trigger. The real Produktberater asks: Hauttyp (7 options), Bedürfnisse (10 options). A gift buyer can't answer either. |
| 4 | No gift wrapping available for a premium-priced product | Pain Point | Purchase | Emerging | **NEEDS MORE INTERVIEWS** — is this blocking purchases or just annoying? (Note: Babor FAQ confirms: "Aktuell ist es leider nicht möglich, Produkte als Geschenk verpacken zu lassen.") |
| 5 | Repeat gift buyers default to safe repurchases because trying something new feels risky | Desire | Loyalty | Emerging | **NEEDS MORE INTERVIEWS** — interesting for repeat rate outcome. How many gift buyers are stuck in this pattern? |

**Reframes performed:**

| Original Statement | Reframed Opportunity |
|-------------------|---------------------|
| "You need a better gift finder" | Non-expert buyers cannot navigate the catalog without skincare knowledge (#1) |
| "I need the version for someone who doesn't know about skincare" | Product descriptions assume skincare literacy (#2) |
| "You should let me gift-wrap it" | No gift presentation option for a premium-priced product (#4) |

Note: This interview was almost entirely solutions and feature requests. Every statement was reframed. This is common with non-primary users who think in terms of "what you should build" rather than describing their experience. The reframes are solid — the underlying needs are real even though they were expressed as demands. The Produktberater finding is particularly actionable because the tool exists — it's the audience assumption behind it that's wrong.

**Memorable Quote:**
> "I'd try something new but I don't want to get it wrong."

**Follow-up Questions for Next Interview:**
- Ask other gift buyers: "Walk me through the last time you bought a gift on our site. What happened?"
- Ask first-time buyers: "When you first visited the site, what did you understand and what confused you?"

## Example 3: Edge Case — Contradiction + Escalation

### Raw Interview Notes

> Interviewed Petra, esthetician, runs a small Babor-certified beauty institute in
> Cologne. Independent operator, not a Babor-owned spa.
>
> "My clients trust me. When I recommend a Babor product, they buy it from me, right
> here in the institute. That's part of my business model."
>
> "But now Babor is pushing the online shop harder. My clients are coming in saying
> 'I found it cheaper on your website.' That's not cheaper — that's MY margin they're
> taking. I do the consultation, I build the relationship, and then Babor gets the
> sale online."
>
> "If this keeps going, I'll start recommending other brands. I'm not going to do
> free marketing for a company that undercuts me."
>
> When asked about the professional portal: "It's fine for ordering. But it doesn't
> help me compete with the online shop. I need Babor to recognize that when a client
> buys online after visiting my institute, I should get credit for that."
>
> "The worst part is the Adventskalender. Every November, my clients buy it online
> instead of from me — Babor even runs a Gewinnspiel (contest) around it that drives
> traffic to the online shop. And the clients earn b.points in the BABOR & me loyalty
> program for buying online. I can't compete with that. It's the same product, but
> the online shop gives them points and I give them expertise."
>
> She also mentioned that two other institute operators in her region feel the same way.
> "We talked about it at the last training. Everyone is frustrated."

### Operator Output

**INTERVIEW SNAPSHOT**

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Interviewee** | Independent esthetician, Babor-certified institute, Cologne |
| **Segment** | Professional channel — independent operator |
| **Context** | Revenue model depends on in-institute product sales; perceives online channel as competitor |

**Experience Map:**
1. Petra consults with client → recommends Babor products
2. Client discovers same product on Babor's online shop → perceives it as cheaper
3. Client buys online → Petra loses the sale but did the consultation work
4. Seasonal promotions (advent calendar) amplify the price gap
5. Petra considers recommending competitor brands to protect her margin

**Extracted Opportunities:**

| # | Opportunity | Type | Phase | Confidence | Route |
|---|------------|------|-------|------------|-------|
| 1 | Independent institute operators lose product sales to Babor's own online channel after doing the consultation work | Pain Point | Loyalty | **Moderate** (Petra + 2 colleagues at training) | **ESCALATE** — this is a channel conflict that could cause professional partners to leave the brand. Directly threatens the professional channel's business model. |
| 2 | No attribution mechanism connecting in-institute consultations to online purchases | Need | Purchase → Loyalty | Emerging | **ESCALATE** — same channel conflict. Routes with #1. |
| 3 | Seasonal promotions (Adventskalender + Gewinnspiel) and the BABOR & me loyalty program (b.points) actively incentivize clients to buy online instead of from their institute | Pain Point | Purchase | Moderate | **ESCALATE** — the loyalty program structurally disadvantages professional partners. This is pricing/channel strategy, not a product team decision. |

**ESCALATION NOTICE**

This interview surfaces a **channel conflict** between Babor's professional partner network and its direct-to-consumer online channel. This is not a product opportunity in the traditional sense — it is a business model tension.

**Why this escalates:**
- Petra explicitly threatened to switch to competitor brands
- She reports this is a shared sentiment among multiple institute operators
- The team's outcome (increase online repeat purchase rate) may be *in direct tension* with professional channel retention
- Resolving this requires decisions above the product team level (pricing strategy, channel attribution, partner economics)

**Who should review:** Product leadership + channel/partnership team

**The contradiction:**
- Example 1 (Maria) wants *more* connection between spa and online → she sees online as an extension of her service
- Example 3 (Petra) wants *less* online competition → she sees online as a threat to her business

These are not the same opportunity. Maria is an employee of a Babor-owned spa. Petra is an independent operator. The segment difference explains the contradiction — their business models are different, so the same "spa-to-online connection" means opposite things to each.

**Do not resolve this contradiction. Surface it.**

**Memorable Quote:**
> "I'm not going to do free marketing for a company that undercuts me."

**Follow-up Questions for Next Interview:**
- Ask other independent operators: "When your clients buy Babor products, where do they buy them? Has that changed in the last year?"
- Ask Babor-employed spa staff: "Do you see online sales as competing with your in-spa sales, or complementing them?"

## What These Examples Show

1. **Example 1** — Standard triage. Multiple opportunities extracted, classified, routed. One reframe (solution → opportunity). The operator decides and routes without asking.

2. **Example 2** — The entire interview was solutions and feature requests. The operator reframed every statement into underlying needs. This is the most common edge case — non-expert users express needs as demands. The operator does not discard these interviews; it translates them.

3. **Example 3** — The operator escalated instead of triaging. Not every interview produces opportunities for the tree. Some reveal business-level tensions that the product team cannot resolve alone. The operator recognized this, escalated clearly, and — critically — connected the contradiction between Example 1 and Example 3 without trying to resolve it. The contradiction IS the finding.
