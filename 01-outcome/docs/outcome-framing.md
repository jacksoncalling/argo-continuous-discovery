# Outcome Framing — Teresa Torres' Criteria

## What Makes a Good Product Outcome

A product outcome measures **a customer behavior in the product** or **a customer sentiment
about the product** (Torres, 2021). It must be:

1. **Directly influenceable** by the product team through product decisions
2. **Measurable** — has a current state and a target state
3. **Customer-facing** — describes what the customer does or feels, not what the business earns
4. **Specific enough to prioritize against** — "improve the experience" is not an outcome

## The Reframing Pattern

| PM says | Problem | Reframe to |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| "Increase revenue by 10%" | Business outcome — team can't control all revenue levers | "Increase repeat purchase rate by 15%" (customer behavior the product influences) |
| "Reduce churn to under 5%" | Business outcome — churn has many causes | "Increase the percentage of users who complete onboarding and use the core feature within 7 days" |
| "Get 50K new signups" | Traction metric — can be bought with ads | "Increase the percentage of first-time visitors who start a free trial" |
| "Improve customer satisfaction" | Too vague — no measurement | "Increase NPS among customers who have used the product for 30+ days from 32 to 45" |

## Outcome Components

Every outcome has two parts:

- **Direction:** What moves? Which way? (e.g., "Increase repeat purchase rate")
- **Goal:** How much? By when? (e.g., "from 22% to 30% by Q3")

The direction is essential. The goal is important but can be refined as the team learns.

## One Outcome Per Tree

Torres is explicit: one Opportunity Solution Tree maps to one product outcome. If the team
has multiple outcomes, they maintain multiple trees. Mixing outcomes in one tree creates
confusion about which opportunities matter — an opportunity that's critical for Outcome A
may be irrelevant for Outcome B.
